I built a tool that simulates thousands of possible future scenarios for a stock instead of trying to predict a single target price.

After a long time working on it, I've finally decided to share one of my personal projects on LinkedIn.

The project started from a simple observation: as retail traders, most of us rely on the same toolbox - RSI, MACD, manually drawn trendlines, and technical analysis concepts shared online. While these tools can be useful, they are often based on information that has already been reflected in the market.

I wanted to explore a different approach.

Rather than focusing only on price action, I started looking at where institutional money might be positioning itself. Today, large market participants often leave footprints through unusual volume, options activity, and momentum shifts before they become obvious on the chart.

So I built a framework that combines three components:
- Monte Carlo simulations to generate thousands of potential price paths and estimate probabilities of future outcomes.
- Market flow analysis to track options activity, momentum, volume, and key support/resistance levels.
- An AI analyst layer that pulls everything together - simulations, flow, indicators, and news - into a clear, structured read instead of a wall of raw numbers.

The goal isn't to predict the future. It's to improve decision-making by understanding probabilities and identifying where capital is moving.

A few recent examples:

- May 20: The tool detected unusual call activity across several quantum computing stocks while they were trading near key demand zones. The following day, the Trump administration announced plans to take approximately $2 billion in equity stakes across the sector. Many of these stocks gained more than 40% within three trading days.

- Last week: It identified significant options activity across semiconductor names such as ARM, AVGO, QCOM, MU, and SNDK. Following Broadcom's earnings release, market conditions shifted and the broader correction that unfolded on Thursday and Friday aligned with the risk signals being detected.

- More recently: While some stocks appeared to be recovering, the flow suggested caution. Heavy put buying in names such as MU, MRVL, and AAPL pointed to a potential bull trap before AAPL sold off following concerns around its AI strategy.

The point is that the signal showed up before the move, not after.

What I've learned from building this project:
- Probabilities are often more useful than predictions.
- Following capital flows can provide insights that price action alone may miss.
- No model is perfect. Risk management matters more than being right.

This remains a personal learning project, not financial advice. Building it has taught me more about markets, data, and uncertainty than I expected.
